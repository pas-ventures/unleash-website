The Real Killer

False positives kill your company.

Every success is the sum of decisions taken on the information you source or receive. Bad info → wrong turn → wasted months → dead runway.

The equation

Quality of Decisions
= Quality of Information

What False Positives Look Like

  • "That's a great idea, you should build it." — friend being polite.
  • "Yes, I'd use that." — hypothetical, not a commitment.
  • "I'd pay for it." — until the credit card comes out.
  • Survey responses without skin in the game.
  • Your own excitement about your idea.

The "Validation Pipeline" Trap.

The Book That Saves Founders

The Mom Test — 4 principles

📖
The Mom Test
Rob Fitzpatrick
Required reading.
Read it this week.

1. Founder = Truth-Seeking Machine

Avoid praise. Avoid validation. Find the answers that make you feel uncomfortable — those are the real ones.

2. Customer-Obsessed > Solution-Obsessed

Nobody cares about your idea. Everyone cares about their problem. Talk about their lived experience, not your product.

3. Each Call = a Learning Event

Open-ended questions. No pitch. You're here to discover, not to convince.

4. The Only Positive Feedback is 💸

LOIs. Signed contracts. Sign-ups. Pre-orders. Words are not signal — money and commitment are.

Stop Asking These

❌ Poor Questions

Hypothetical. Polite-answer magnets. Noise, not signal.

The questions

  • "Would you use this?"
  • "Do you think this is a good idea?"
  • "Would you pay for this?"
  • "How much would you pay for this?"
  • "What features would you like?"
  • "If we built X, would that solve it?"

Why they fail

  • It's a guess, not a fact.
  • People are polite — they'll say yes.
  • Hypothetical and socially pressured.
  • People don't know until they pay.
  • You're outsourcing product design.
  • Leading the witness.
Guest Session · /*Darkies_in_Tech*/
Ask These Instead

✅ Strong Questions

Anchored in real behaviour. They surface pain, budget, and who actually decides.

The questions

  • "Tell me about the last time you did [X]?"
  • "How are you currently solving this?"
  • "What tools are you using today?"
  • "What's the hardest part about [X]?"
  • "Why did you stop using [other solution]?"
  • "Who else is involved in this process?"

What they reveal

  • Anchored in reality — real workflow.
  • Surfaces alternatives + pain level.
  • Exposes budget, process, habits.
  • Pain and priorities, clearly.
  • Frustrations & switching triggers.
  • Maps buying roles + internal org.

From Talk → Test

The Validation Ladder

Skin in the game = right info. Not all answers are valued equally.

1

Problem Interview

Open conversation. No pitch. Just listen.

30 minutes of their time. Aim for the real workflow, real pain, real alternatives.

Recent founding investment — example

"Walk me through your last 3 mornings — what happened?"

2

Solution Sketch

Mock · wireframe · one-pager. Then open-ended follow-ups — never yes/no.

Their attention + opinion. Aim for substitution behaviour — what they'd actually swap out.

Recent founding investment — example

Show 3-screen mock. Then: "Walk me through how you'd use this in your morning."

3

💸 Demand Test

Pitch · landing page · qualify hard · LOI / deposit.

Their money or reputation. Aim for whether they actually buy.

Recent founding investment — example

Pitch → landing page → leave a number → qualify hard → LOI / deposit.

4

MVP / Workflow Commit

Post-deal use — they commit time AND money.

Behaviour change. Aim for retention, repeat use, referrals.

Recent founding investment — example

Once signed: usage week-on-week, retention, peer referrals.

Designing Experiments

What is a good test?

Four rules. If a test misses any of them, it's theatre — not evidence.

1

Skin in the game

The other side has to spend something — money, time, reputation, calendar block. No skin = no signal.

Example. One €500 deposit beats 100 "I'd love this" replies on a Typeform.

2

Falsifiable

Define what counts as a "no" before you run it. If every outcome confirms your hypothesis, it's not a test.

Example. "If fewer than 3 of 10 SMBs sign the deposit — we kill the wedge." Said out loud. Before launch.

3

Pre-defined success metric

Decide the number that proves it works in advance. "It went well" is not a metric.

Example. "8 of 10 sign within 14 days = green light. 7 or fewer = re-design." Number written down. No moving the goalposts.

4

Committed timeline

Set the cut-off. "We'll see" turns into 6 months of polite emails. Two weeks. Two months. Decide.

Example. "We launch the demand test on Monday the 12th. We decide by Monday the 26th. Either way."

Try this now: take your most recent test — benchmark it against these four. If it misses any, you ran theatre, not a test.

When Pipelines Go Wrong

Pitfalls

📋 Surveys without skin
100 hypothetical responses are worth less than one signed LOI.
Seen this week: founder showed me a 200-response Typeform with 80% "yes." None of them have ever paid for anything similar.
🤝 Friend bias
Your network will tell you it's brilliant. They love you. They're not the customer.
Watch out: if your first 10 conversations were all with people who'd answer your WhatsApp on a Sunday — you have noise, not data.
🛠️ Builder bias
You spent a year on this. You will find evidence it works. That's not validation, that's confirmation.
Tell: you're presenting your roadmap on the call. You're not validating — you're pitching.
💰 Premature pricing
Asking "what would you pay?" before they want it gives you fiction priced as data.
Test: until somebody signs a deposit, every price quote is a guess — theirs and yours.
📈 Scaling the wrong thing
10 great conversations > 50 mediocre ones. Don't industrialise before you've found the truth.
Symptom: hiring a BDR before product-market fit. Building a CRM dashboard before you have 10 buyers to track.
